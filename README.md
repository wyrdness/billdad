## Overview

The Billdad (mock-Latin name Saltipiscator falcorostratus) is a fearsome critter from the tall-tale tradition of North American lumber camps. The creature is described and illustrated by William T. Cox in Fearsome Creatures of the Lumberwoods (1910), the canonical source for the genre. According to Cox, the Billdad lives only around Boundary Pond in northwestern Maine. About the size of a beaver, it has long kangaroo-like hind legs, short forelegs, webbed feet, and a heavy hawk-like bill. It hunts by waiting until a fish breaks the surface to seize an insect, then leaping — reportedly up to 60 yards (about 55 metres) — to land beside it and stun it with a tail-slap before swallowing the prey. Cox's text closes with the comic anecdote of Bill Murphy, a tote-road swamper from Ambegegis, who tasted Billdad stew at a Great Northern Paper Company camp on Hurricane Lake and immediately leapt fifty yards into the lake in imitation of the creature, sinking like a stone. As with the rest of the fearsome critters, the Billdad is a piece of literary folklore — an entertainment, not a serious cryptozoological claim — that lumber-camp storytellers used to play tricks on greenhorns.

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Literary tall-tale tradition** _(Loren Coleman, Robert E. Gard (Wisconsin Lore))_

The Billdad is a piece of authored folklore, partly drawn from oral camp tradition and partly invented by Cox. It is not a zoological hypothesis.

### Skeptical

**Authored folklore — no zoological reality** _(Mainstream folklorists)_

There is no field evidence, specimen, or pre-1910 attestation; the Billdad's behaviour (60-yard standing leaps) is biomechanically impossible at the stated body size.
